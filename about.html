I'm Not Dying Today

Husband. Father. Fighter. Bodybuilder. 4+ years past my diagnosis and still walking.

Before ALS

I was born and raised in California. I was a bodybuilder in high school and college, and I taught martial arts. I have always been active, always physical. That's who I am.

In 1997, my wife Josiane and I relocated to Colorado, where we raised our three boys. I spent my career in automotive paint sales. Life was good. I was strong, I was healthy, and I never slowed down.

When Everything Changed

October 2019. I was on a hunting trip and I kept tripping and falling. Over and over. I knew something was wrong.

By December 2019, I had surgery for a collapsed L5 and S1 in Colorado Springs. But the surgery didn't explain what was really happening to my body.

Over the next two years, I saw 9 specialists. I had 12 MRIs, 3 brain scans, blood panels, CAT scans, X-rays, and a spinal tap. Nobody could tell me what was wrong. Nothing showed up. Two years of searching and no answers.

Then in September 2021, a doctor at Anschutz Hospital in Aurora, Colorado looked at me and said, "I am 90% certain you have ALS."

My wife Josiane knew exactly what that meant. I did not.

They gave me 2 to 5 years. They told me 10% of ALS patients live to 10 years. Only 5% live longer than that.

I decided right then that I would be in the 5%.

Their Way

I did what patients are supposed to do. I entered the Courage ALS clinical trial and stayed in it for a year. I continued the drug protocol for two more months after that, until I got a phone call telling me to stop. It doesn't work.

During that entire period, I was falling apart. I was extremely weak. My speech was slurred. I could barely move. I was dragging my feet. My knees were hyper-extending. I couldn't sit in a car for more than 30 minutes. My ankles were collapsing. My hands and grip were weak. I had no energy. I was using walking sticks and a walker just to get around.

The traditional ALS medical protocol was not working. Not for me.

My Way

So I started doing my own research. Obsessively. I read about alternative supplements, treatments, and protocols. I talked to other patients. I studied everything I could find.

And I insisted on weight training. My doctors told me I couldn't build muscle with ALS. I didn't buy it.

I started slowly. In the beginning, I couldn't leg press more than 50 pounds. Today, I leg press 200 pounds for 4 sets of 15. I exercise 1.5 to 2 hours a day, 5 to 6 days a week.

I am currently taking 23 supplements. Vitamins, plant-based supplements, bodybuilding supplements, and clinical trial supplements. I built this protocol one supplement at a time, one study at a time.

The results speak for themselves. My hamstrings don't hurt anymore. My ankles are rated 5 and they don't collapse. My knees don't snap back on stairs. My balance has improved. I carry on conversations with everyone. At the gym, people tell me I'm a hero and an inspiration.

"MY WAY is not for everyone, but I do believe if you're diagnosed early on, some of my ideas may help... if you are open to stepping out of the box."

Josiane

I need to talk about my wife. Because none of this happens without her.

Josiane and I have been married 45 years. We met in California, raised three boys together in Colorado, built a life. When ALS showed up, she did not flinch. Not once.

She is my primary caregiver, and I need people to understand what that actually means. It means she fills my pill organizer every Sunday. 23 supplements, sorted by time of day, morning and evening. It means she tracks what needs to be reordered and when. She sets up auto-delivery where she can and manually orders the rest. She drives me to every appointment. She talks to the insurance company. She talks to the pharmacy. She makes sure I get to the gym on the days I do not feel like going, which is more often than I would admit to anyone else.

On the hard days, and there are hard days, she is the one who brings me back. She does not let me stay down. She has this way of looking at me that says "we are not done yet" without saying a word.

People talk about the ALS patient. They should talk about the caregiver. Josiane carries a weight that nobody sees. She does it with grace and she does it every single day. 45 years of marriage, and she is still the strongest person I know.

"If you are a caregiver reading this, I see you. What you are doing matters more than you know. Your person could not fight without you."

I thank God for her every day.

In My Own Words

People ask me how I stay positive. I'm not always positive. But these are the things I tell myself and every ALS patient I talk to:

"I am not dying from ALS. I will die from something else. Dying is not an option."

"You have to believe in yourself. Believe you are going to do this."

"Remember this: people break barriers every day. Why not you."

"I live every day to live, not to die."

"Every single day you are at the gym is a shot into ALS. You will not take me. Not going to happen."

"Newly diagnosed, remember this: you are not dead yet. Your journey is just beginning. Make it on your terms and make it happen."

People with ALS know that others really do not understand what we're going through on the inside. We have two choices: give up and give in, or stand and fight. I have chosen to stand and fight.

Why I Share This

For years, I've been sharing my supplement list informally. Over phone calls, in support groups, at doctor's visits. Other ALS patients ask me what I take, and I tell them. Many of them start taking the same supplements.

This site exists to make that information available to every ALS patient who wants it, not just the ones who happen to know me personally.

"We have two choices: give up and give in. Or stand and fight. I have chosen to stand and fight."

And when people ask me, how does it feel to be dying, I simply stand as tall as possible and reply:

I'M NOT DYING TODAY.
