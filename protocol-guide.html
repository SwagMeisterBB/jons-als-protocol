Section 1

Why This Protocol Exists

In October 2019, I was on a hunting trip when I started tripping and falling for no reason. That was the beginning. Two months later I had surgery on a collapsed L5/S1 disc. Then came two years of searching for answers: 9 specialists, 12 MRIs, 3 brain scans. Nobody could tell me what was wrong.

In September 2021, at Anschutz Hospital in Aurora, Colorado, I got my answer: ALS.

I entered the Courage ALS clinical trial for a year. The drug did not work. After the trial ended, I went into a steep decline. My speech was slurring, my legs and ankles were getting weaker, my hands were losing grip, and I was using a walker.

That is when I decided to do this my way. I had been a bodybuilder in high school and college. I was a martial arts instructor. I knew what it meant to push a body. So I went to work. Thousands of hours of research on PubMed. Conversations with Dr. Richard Bedlack, the director of the Duke ALS program. And I built this protocol, supplement by supplement, based on what the research actually showed.

"I am not dying from ALS. I will die from something else. Dying is not an option."

Jon Edy

Today I leg press 200 pounds, 4 sets of 15 reps. When I started, I could barely press 50. I exercise 1.5 to 2 hours a day, 5 to 6 days a week. I am still walking. And I am sharing everything I have learned so that other patients and caregivers do not have to piece it together alone.

This guide is everything. Every supplement, every brand, every dose, every cost. The daily timing. The protein shake recipe. The mindset. All of it.

Section 3

The Daily Protein Shake

I make this every single morning. It is the foundation of my day and it carries a huge chunk of my L-Serine and Lion's Mane doses. It takes about 3 minutes.

Jon's Protocol Shake

  • Orgain Protein Powder — 2 scoops (24g protein)
  • L-Serine — 4 scoops (13,600 mg)
  • Lion's Mane Powder — 1 scoop (2,000 mg)
  • Berries — blueberries, strawberries, whatever is around
  • Spinach — a handful, you do not taste it
  • Water or almond milk — enough to blend smooth

Blend it all together. The L-Serine dissolves clean and has no taste. The Lion's Mane powder adds a slight earthiness but the berries cover it completely. I drink this first thing after my morning supplements.

Section 5

My Top 3 and Why

If someone asked me "if you could only take three supplements, what would they be?" the answer is immediate. These three are the core of everything I do.

  1. HMB (6,000 mg/day) I was a bodybuilder. I know what HMB does for muscle preservation. When ALS is attacking your muscles every single day, anything that slows breakdown is worth its weight in gold. I take it 30 minutes before I train, every time. Dr. Bedlack showed interest in this one.
  2. L-Serine (30,600 mg/day) This is the one with the most direct ALS research behind it. A Phase 1 clinical trial showed patients taking L-Serine had roughly 85% slower functional decline. Dr. Bedlack guided me on this. It is also the most expensive supplement in my stack, but it is the one I would never drop.
  3. Basis / NAD+ (8 tablets/day) NAD+ is the molecule your cells need to produce energy. It declines as you age and declines faster with neurodegeneration. Basis from Elysium contains nicotinamide riboside and pterostilbene, both NAD+ precursors. Dr. Bedlack guided me on this one too. Elysium gives me a 50% discount.

Every other supplement in my stack supports these three in some way, whether it is reducing inflammation, supporting mitochondrial function, protecting the liver from the supplement load, or feeding the muscles I am fighting to keep.

Section 8

A Note for Caregivers

My wife Josiane has been by my side for 45 years. She is the one who organizes the pills, keeps the schedule, orders the refills, and makes sure nothing falls through the cracks. If you are reading this guide for someone you love, I see you. You are doing something that takes incredible strength.

A few practical notes for caregivers managing this protocol:

  • Use a weekly pill organizer. This many supplements requires a system. Josiane fills mine every Sunday.
  • Set up auto-delivery where possible. Amazon Subscribe & Save, Elysium auto-ship, etc. Running out of a supplement breaks the routine.
  • Keep a simple spreadsheet tracking what is ordered, when it arrives, and when it runs out. The supplement data in Section 2 of this guide is a good starting template.
  • Talk to the patient's neurologist before adding anything. Bring this guide to the appointment. Doctors respond better when you show up prepared.
  • Take care of yourself too. You cannot pour from an empty cup. This is a marathon, not a sprint.
Section 9

Working with Your Neurologist

I am fortunate to work with Dr. Richard Bedlack, who directs the Duke ALS Program. He is one of the most respected ALS researchers in the world, and he has been open to discussing supplements with me, which is not always the case with neurologists.

Dr. Bedlack specifically guided me on L-Serine and Basis, and showed interest in HMB. That does not mean he endorses everything on this list, and it does not mean your neurologist will agree with any of it. But it does mean that having these conversations with your doctor is worth the effort.

Bring data. Bring PubMed links. Be respectful of their expertise. And be persistent. You are your own best advocate.
