Omega-3 Fish Oil for ALS: Fighting Neuroinflammation

Jon EdyMarch 29, 2026 · 5 min read

In this article

  2. What the Research Says
  3. How I Take It

Neuroinflammation is not a side effect of ALS. It is one of the engines driving it. The inflammatory cascade in the central nervous system accelerates motor neuron death, and anything I can do to dampen that cascade is worth serious consideration. That is why I take high-dose fish oil every single day.

I have spent years reading the research on omega-3 fatty acids and neurodegenerative disease. The evidence is not ALS-specific in the way L-Serine data is, but the anti-inflammatory mechanism is well established and the safety profile is excellent. For me, the risk-reward calculation is straightforward.

Why I Take Fish Oil

The omega-3 fatty acids EPA and DHA are the most studied anti-inflammatory nutrients in existence. They work by competing with omega-6 fatty acids for the same enzymatic pathways. Most Americans consume far too much omega-6 (from seed oils and processed food) and too little omega-3. This imbalance promotes chronic inflammation.

In ALS, neuroinflammation is not hypothetical. It is measurable. Inflammatory markers are elevated in the cerebrospinal fluid of ALS patients. Microglia, the immune cells of the brain, become chronically activated. Reducing systemic inflammation will not cure ALS, but it may slow one of the processes contributing to progression. That is enough reason for me.

What the Research Says

Fitzgerald et al. (2014) published a large prospective study analyzing dietary data from over one million participants. They found that higher intake of omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids was associated with a reduced risk of developing ALS. This was not a small effect, and it held up after adjusting for confounders.

In preclinical work, EPA and DHA have been shown to reduce inflammatory markers in ALS models. The mechanism is well understood: omega-3s are converted into specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs) that actively resolve inflammation, rather than just suppressing it. This is a cleaner anti-inflammatory approach than most drugs.

I want to be clear: there is no randomized controlled trial proving fish oil slows ALS progression. But the anti-inflammatory mechanism is rock solid, the safety data spans decades, and the deficiency in the typical Western diet is well documented.

How I Take It

  • Form: High-concentration fish oil capsules or liquid. I look for products that list the actual EPA and DHA content, not just "fish oil."
  • Dose: 520 mg of Wholemega fish oil per day. This is higher than what most bottles recommend, but it is within the range studied for anti-inflammatory effects.
  • Timing: Split between morning and evening, always with meals. Taking it with food improves absorption and reduces the fishy aftertaste.
  • Quality: I only buy fish oil with third-party purity testing. IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards) certification is what I look for. This ensures low mercury, low oxidation, and accurate label claims.
  • Cost: About $25 to $40 per month depending on the brand and concentration.

Important Notes

  • Blood thinning. Fish oil has a mild blood-thinning effect at high doses. If you are on anticoagulants like warfarin, talk to your doctor before starting high-dose fish oil.
  • Quality matters more than brand. Cheap fish oil can be oxidized (rancid), which is counterproductive. If your fish oil smells strongly of fish, it may be oxidized. Look for IFOS certification or a similar third-party testing seal.
  • GI tolerance. Some people get stomach upset from fish oil. Starting with a lower dose and taking it with food usually solves this. Enteric-coated capsules can also help.
  • This is one piece of my protocol. Fish oil alone will not change the trajectory of ALS. But as part of a comprehensive anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective strategy, I believe it contributes. I have been taking it for over 6 years.
