Most people know creatine as a bodybuilding supplement. Walk into any gym in America and you will find a tub of it in someone's bag. But when I was diagnosed with ALS more than six years ago, I started looking at creatine very differently.

ALS destroys motor neurons. When those neurons die, the muscles they control waste away. You lose the ability to move, to speak, to swallow, to breathe. There is no cure. So when I started building my supplement protocol, I asked a simple question about every single thing I considered: does this have a plausible mechanism for helping my muscles hold on longer?

Creatine cleared that bar. Here is why.

Why I Take Creatine

Creatine is one of the most well-studied supplements in existence. In healthy people, it helps muscles produce energy during high-intensity exercise. It does this by increasing the availability of phosphocreatine in your cells, which your body uses to regenerate ATP, the basic energy currency of every cell in your body.

Now think about what ALS does. It puts your motor neurons and muscles under enormous stress. The cells that are still working are fighting to stay alive with less and less support. Anything that helps maintain cellular energy, that helps those remaining muscle cells function more efficiently, seemed worth trying to me.

I am not claiming creatine slows ALS progression, but the logic made sense to me: my muscles are under siege, creatine helps muscles maintain energy, and it has decades of safety data behind it. That was enough for me to add it to my daily routine and keep it there for six-plus years.

What the Research Says

I want to be straightforward here, because creatine has actually been studied specifically in ALS, and the clinical trial results were disappointing.

In 2003, Groeneveld and colleagues published a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in the journal Neurology. They gave ALS patients 10 grams of creatine per day for 16 months. The result: no significant difference in survival or functional decline compared to placebo.

In 2004, Shefner and colleagues published another randomized controlled trial, also in Neurology. Same story, creatine at 5 grams twice daily did not show a statistically significant benefit on the ALS Functional Rating Scale.

So why do I still take it? A few reasons:

  • The mechanism still makes sense. Clinical trials measure group averages. The fact that creatine did not show a statistically significant benefit in a small trial does not mean it does nothing at the cellular level. ATP production and cellular energy are real, measurable things.
  • The safety profile is excellent. Creatine has been studied for decades in athletes and the general population. At standard doses, side effects are minimal. For me, the risk-to-reward ratio is very favorable.
  • It is inexpensive and easy. Creatine monohydrate costs pennies per day. Adding it to my protocol is essentially free compared to many other supplements.
  • Preclinical data was encouraging. Before the human trials, animal studies in ALS models showed creatine improved motor function and extended survival in mice. The translation to humans did not pan out in the trials we have, but the underlying biology is sound.

I am a realist, and I know the clinical evidence is not there for creatine as an ALS treatment. But I also know that ALS trials are notoriously difficult, with small sample sizes, heterogeneous disease progression, and short durations, so I weigh all of that when I make my own decisions.

How I Take It

My creatine routine is about as simple as it gets:

  • Dose: 5 grams of creatine monohydrate per day
  • Timing: Morning, mixed into water or a smoothie
  • Brand: Any reputable creatine monohydrate. This is a commodity supplement, the raw ingredient is the same across brands. I typically buy whatever is well-reviewed and reasonably priced on Amazon.
  • Loading phase: I do not do one. Some people take 20 grams per day for a week to "load" their muscles with creatine. Research shows this speeds up saturation, but at 5 grams per day you will reach the same levels in about three to four weeks. I saw no reason to rush.

Creatine monohydrate dissolves reasonably well in water and is basically tasteless, so you will not even notice it in a smoothie or mixed drink. Of all the supplements in my protocol, creatine is one of the easiest to take consistently.

Important Notes

I need to be clear about a few things:

  • Creatine is not FDA-approved for ALS. It is sold as a dietary supplement for general fitness. No regulatory body has approved it as a treatment for any neurodegenerative disease.
  • I am not a doctor. I am an ALS patient who has lived with this disease for more than six years. Everything on this site is my personal experience and my personal reasoning. It is not medical advice.
  • Talk to your physician. Before adding creatine or any supplement to your routine, have a conversation with your neurologist or primary care doctor. They know your specific situation, your medications, and your health history.
  • Stay hydrated. Creatine pulls water into your muscle cells. This is one of its mechanisms of action, but it means you should drink plenty of water throughout the day.
  • This is one piece of the puzzle. Creatine is one of roughly 20 supplements I take daily. No single supplement is a silver bullet. If you want the complete picture, check out the full protocol.

I have been taking creatine every single day for years, and honestly I cannot isolate the effect of any one supplement in my stack. What I can tell you is that I am still here, still walking, still fighting, and that is enough for me to keep going.
