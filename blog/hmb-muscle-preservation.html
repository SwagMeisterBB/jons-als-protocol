HMB for Muscle Preservation: A Bodybuilding Supplement for ALS?

Jon Edy, ALS Patient & Protocol Author

Most people know HMB (Beta-Hydroxy Beta-Methylbutyrate) as a bodybuilding supplement. It is a metabolite of leucine, an essential amino acid, and it has been studied extensively for its ability to reduce muscle protein breakdown.

For someone with ALS, muscle breakdown is the core problem because motor neurons die, muscles stop receiving signals, and they atrophy. When I was putting together my supplement protocol, anything that could slow that breakdown was worth investigating, and HMB cleared that bar.

Why I Take HMB

The logic is straightforward: if HMB reduces muscle breakdown in healthy athletes, it may help preserve muscle in ALS patients whose muscles are wasting due to denervation.

There is limited ALS-specific clinical data on HMB. Most of the research comes from the sports nutrition and geriatric medicine worlds, where HMB has been shown to:

  • Reduce muscle protein breakdown
  • Support lean muscle mass in older adults
  • Help preserve strength during periods of inactivity or bed rest

That last point is especially relevant to me. ALS forces periods of reduced activity as the disease progresses. Having something in my stack that helps preserve muscle during those periods, even marginally, seemed like a reasonable bet.

I consider HMB one of my "muscle support" supplements, and while it is not a magic bullet, it is a reasonable addition to my protocol based on the available research.

What the Research Says

I want to be upfront: there are no large-scale clinical trials testing HMB specifically in ALS patients, so the evidence I rely on comes from adjacent fields.

In the sports nutrition world, HMB has decades of research behind it. A 2003 meta-analysis by Nissen and Sharp in the Journal of Applied Physiology found that HMB supplementation at 6 grams per day significantly reduced muscle protein breakdown and improved lean body mass gains during resistance training.

In geriatric medicine, HMB has shown promise for older adults experiencing sarcopenia, age-related muscle loss. A 2013 study by Wu and colleagues published in Clinical Nutrition found that HMB supplementation helped preserve muscle mass in older adults during periods of bed rest.

The mechanism is what matters to me. HMB works by inhibiting the ubiquitin-proteasome pathway, which is one of the main systems your body uses to break down muscle protein. It also stimulates muscle protein synthesis through the mTOR pathway. Both of those mechanisms are relevant to what is happening in ALS, my muscles are being broken down, and anything that tips the balance toward preservation is worth trying.

Do I know for certain it is helping? No, but the safety profile is excellent, the cost is low, and the underlying biology makes sense, which is my threshold for including something in my protocol.

How I Take It

My HMB routine is simple:

  • Dose: 6 grams per day, which is the dose most commonly used in research studies
  • Timing: Split across the day with meals, typically 1 gram three times per day
  • Form: Capsules. I find them easier to stay consistent with than powder
  • Consistency: Every single day. HMB is not something you cycle on and off. The research uses continuous daily dosing

Of all the supplements in my protocol, HMB is one of the easiest to take, just three capsules spread across the day with food, no mixing, no taste issues, no complicated timing.

Important Notes

I need to be clear about a few things:

  • HMB is not FDA-approved for ALS. It is sold as a dietary supplement. No regulatory body has approved it as a treatment for any neurodegenerative disease.
  • Most HMB research is in healthy populations. The studies I referenced above were conducted in athletes and older adults, not ALS patients specifically. I am extrapolating from the mechanism of action.
  • I am not a doctor. I am an ALS patient who has lived with this disease for more than six years. Everything on this site is my personal experience and my personal reasoning. It is not medical advice.
  • Talk to your physician. Before adding HMB or any supplement to your routine, have a conversation with your neurologist or primary care doctor. They know your specific situation, your medications, and your health history.
  • This is one piece of the puzzle. HMB is one of roughly 20 supplements I take daily. No single supplement is a silver bullet. If you want the complete picture, check out the full protocol.

I have been taking HMB every single day as part of my protocol, and honestly I cannot isolate the effect of any one supplement in my stack. What I can tell you is that I am still here, still walking, still fighting, and that is enough for me to keep going.
