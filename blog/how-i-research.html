How I Built This Protocol: Thousands of Hours of Research

Jon Edy, ALS Patient & Protocol Author

March 29, 2026 • 7 min read

People ask me how I decide what to take. They want to know if I just follow what other ALS patients do, or if I listen to supplement companies, or if I found a list somewhere online. The answer is none of the above. I built this protocol the hard way. I taught myself to read clinical research, I spent thousands of hours on PubMed, and I developed a framework for evaluating every single compound that crosses my radar. This post explains exactly how I do it.

I Am Not a Scientist. But I Read Like One.

When I was diagnosed with ALS, I had a choice: I could accept the standard prognosis and wait, or I could fight. Fighting meant understanding the disease at a level most patients never reach, and it meant learning to read the same papers my neurologist reads.

I taught myself study design. I learned the difference between a Phase 1 safety trial and a Phase 3 efficacy trial. I learned what statistical significance actually means, and more importantly, what it does not mean. I learned about effect sizes, confidence intervals, p-values, and why a study with 20 participants tells you almost nothing no matter how impressive the results look. I learned to spot the difference between a randomized controlled trial and an observational study, and why that distinction matters enormously when you are deciding whether to put something in your body.

I did not go to medical school and I do not have a PhD. But I have spent thousands of hours reading primary research on ALS, and I have developed a critical eye for the difference between real evidence and marketing dressed up as science. Most supplement companies are selling hope, and I am looking for data.

Where I Find My Information

My sources are specific and I am deliberate about what I trust.

What I use:

  • PubMed for clinical studies and peer-reviewed research. This is my primary source. If a claim does not have a PubMed citation behind it, I treat it as an opinion.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov for ongoing and completed ALS trials. This tells me what researchers are actually testing right now, which is a strong signal of where the science is heading.
  • ALS patient forums and communities for real-world experience. Other patients can tell me what they have actually experienced with a supplement. That is valuable context, not evidence, but context.
  • Conversations with my neurologist. I bring research to my appointments. We discuss it. He tells me what concerns him and what he is comfortable with.
  • Conversations with other long-term ALS survivors. People who have lived with this disease for 5, 10, 15 years have insights that no study can capture.

What I do NOT use as primary sources:

  • Supplement company marketing materials
  • Influencer recommendations
  • Health blogs
  • Reddit threads

I want to be clear: I am not saying those sources are worthless. A Reddit thread might point me toward a compound I have not considered, and a health blog might summarize a study I have not seen. But they are starting points, not evidence. I always go back to the primary source, and if I cannot find a peer-reviewed paper supporting a claim, I do not act on it.

How I Evaluate a Supplement

Every supplement that crosses my radar goes through the same five questions. If it cannot pass all five, it does not make the protocol. No exceptions.

  1. Is there a plausible biological mechanism relevant to ALS? The supplement needs to target something that actually matters in ALS: oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, neuroinflammation, excitotoxicity, protein aggregation. If the mechanism is vague or unrelated to motor neuron disease, it is out.
  2. Is there at least preclinical data? I need to see animal studies, in vitro work, or ideally human trials showing the compound does something relevant. A theoretical mechanism is not enough. Show me data.
  3. What is the safety profile, and are there known interactions? I take roughly 20 supplements. Interactions matter. Side effects matter. If something has a narrow therapeutic window or dangerous interactions with other compounds in my stack, it is too risky regardless of potential benefit.
  4. What is the realistic monthly cost? I am building a protocol I can sustain for years. A supplement that costs $200 per month needs dramatically better evidence than one that costs $15. Cost is a practical filter that keeps the protocol sustainable.
  5. Is my neurologist comfortable with me taking it? This is the final check. My neurologist, Dr. Richard Bedlack at Duke, knows my full medical picture. If he has concerns about a supplement, I listen. This does not mean he has to enthusiastically endorse everything. But if he says "I would rather you not take that," I respect it.

Let me give you concrete examples of how this works in practice.

L-Serine: Passed all five. The mechanism is relevant. There is a Phase 1 clinical trial in ALS patients showing it is safe and suggesting possible benefit. The safety profile is excellent. It costs about $30 per month. My neurologist at Duke supports it. It made the protocol.

Clenbuterol: Failed point three. There is interesting preclinical data on clenbuterol and motor neurons. But the cardiac risks are significant. It can cause arrhythmias, tachycardia, and other cardiovascular problems. For someone already dealing with ALS, adding cardiac risk is not a trade I am willing to make. It did not make the cut.

Turmeric/Curcumin: Failed point two. The preclinical data on curcumin looks promising on paper. Anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective in cell cultures. But here is the problem: oral curcumin has terrible bioavailability. Most of what you swallow never reaches your bloodstream at meaningful levels. Despite decades of research, there are essentially no convincing clinical trials showing oral curcumin does what the in vitro studies suggest. The preclinical data does not translate because the compound cannot get where it needs to go. Promising mechanism, useless delivery. Out.

Why I Share What I Found

I did not build this site because I enjoy writing. I built it because I remember what it was like in the early days after my diagnosis: the late-night Google searches, the forums full of conflicting opinions, the supplement companies promising miracles, and the desperation of wanting to do something, anything, while having no framework for evaluating what actually makes sense.

Every newly diagnosed ALS patient goes through this. They find the same confusing landscape I found, read the same marketing disguised as medical information, and join the same forums where someone swears by a compound that has zero clinical evidence. And they have no way to tell the difference between a reasonable supplement and expensive wishful thinking.

I spent thousands of hours doing this research so you do not have to. That does not mean you should blindly follow my protocol. It means you should have access to the distilled output of someone who has read the papers, evaluated the evidence, and built a framework for making these decisions. Take what is useful, bring it to your doctor, and make your own informed choices.

This protocol is not perfect. I update it when new evidence emerges. I drop supplements that no longer make sense and add new ones when the data supports it. But the framework does not change. The five questions are the same whether I am evaluating something for the first time or re-evaluating something I have been taking for years. That consistency is what separates a protocol from a pile of bottles on a shelf.
