This Disease Will Not Kill Me

When they told me I had two to five years, I decided something right there in that doctor's office: this disease will not kill me. I don't know if that's true. But I wake up every morning and choose it. That choice, that daily, deliberate choice, is the foundation of everything else on this site.

I want to be clear about something, because this is not denial. I know what ALS is, and I have watched friends die from it. I have sat in support groups where the chair next to me was empty the following month. I know the statistics, I know the progression, and I know what the textbooks say.

But I refuse to accept a timeline.

There is a difference between denial and defiance. Denial is pretending the disease does not exist. Defiance is looking it square in the face and saying: I see you, I understand what you are, and I am going to fight you with everything I have for as long as I have it. I do not live in denial. I live in defiance.

That distinction matters more than almost anything else I can tell you, because denial will get you killed since you will not take action, while defiance will keep you alive because it demands action. Every supplement I take, every exercise I do, every protocol I follow, it all starts with the decision that I am not done yet.

My 5 Rules

Over the years, I have developed a mental framework. Five rules. They are not complicated. They are not philosophical. They are just the things I have learned to tell myself every single day to keep going.

Rule 1: I don't let anyone tell me my timeline.

Doctors give averages. I am not average. Nobody is. When a neurologist says "two to five years," they are describing a median based on population data. Half the people in those studies lived longer than that. Some lived much longer. I respect medicine, I rely on it, but I refuse to internalize a statistic as my destiny. The moment you accept a timeline, you start living to it. I decided early on that I would not give my brain that target.

Rule 2: I treat every day like I'm building something.

This is not about surviving, because surviving is passive. I am building a protocol, building a routine, building this site so other people can benefit from what I have learned. Purpose is not some self-help abstraction, it is the thing that gets me out of bed on the mornings when my body does not want to cooperate. When you are building something you have a reason to show up tomorrow, and that reason matters more than you think.

Rule 3: I refuse to be a statistic.

ALS statistics describe populations, not individuals. They tell you what happened to a group of people under specific conditions in specific studies at a specific point in time. They do not tell you what will happen to you. I focus on what I can control: what I put in my body, how I move, how I think, who I surround myself with. The statistics are someone else's story. I am writing my own.

Rule 4: I do the work even when I don't feel like it.

I take roughly 20 supplements every day, I exercise, and I stick to my routine. Some days it is exhausting, and some days my hands do not cooperate, and some days the fatigue is so heavy that the idea of measuring out powders and swallowing pills feels impossible. I do it anyway, not because I am tough, but because the alternative, giving up on the routine, letting the days slide, telling myself I will start again tomorrow, is how you lose ground you cannot get back. Discipline is not motivation, it is what you do when motivation is gone.

Rule 5: I stay connected.

Isolation kills faster than any disease, and I have seen it happen. ALS patients who withdraw from family, who stop talking to friends, who close themselves off because they are embarrassed or exhausted or depressed, they decline faster. I am not making a clinical claim, I am just telling you what I have observed. I stay engaged with my family, I talk to other ALS patients, and I share my protocol with anyone who wants to hear it, because connection is not a luxury when you are fighting a terminal illness. It is a survival tool.

What This Looks Like in Practice

I do not want this to sound abstract, so let me tell you what it actually looks like.

Some mornings my hands do not work well and the fine motor control is not what it used to be. On those mornings, measuring out my L-Serine takes longer than it should because the scoop does not cooperate and the powder spills. A healthy person would knock it out in thirty seconds, but it takes me five minutes. I do it anyway, because that is Rule 4, and I do the work even when my body makes it hard.

Some days, the fatigue is crushing. Not tired, crushing. The kind of fatigue where your muscles feel like they are filled with wet sand and your brain is telling you that the couch is where you belong for the rest of the day. On those days, I still walk. Maybe not as far. Maybe not as fast. But I get up and I move. Because I know from six-plus years of experience that the days I give in to the fatigue are the days I lose a little more. And I am not willing to give anything up without a fight.

I get phone calls from other ALS patients. People who found this site, or who heard about my protocol through a support group, or whose caregiver reached out on their behalf. Those calls are not easy. Sometimes the person on the other end is scared. Sometimes they are angry. Sometimes they just want someone to tell them that it is possible to still be here years after diagnosis. I take those calls because that is Rule 5. Staying connected is not just about what I get from it. It is about what I can give.

And every single day, I come back to Rule 2. I am building something. This site, this protocol, this record of what has worked for me, it is bigger than just my fight. If even one person reads this and decides to reject their timeline, decides to build their own protocol, decides to keep going when everything is telling them to stop, then the work mattered.

For the Person Who Just Got Diagnosed

If you just got that call, or you are sitting in the parking lot of the neurologist's office reading this on your phone, I want you to know something.

What they told you is a median, not a sentence.

I am proof that the timeline is not fixed. I was told two to five years, and I am still here, still walking, still fighting. I am not special or genetically gifted or lucky. I just made a decision and I stuck with it.

You are going to feel a lot of things in the coming days. Fear. Anger. Grief. Confusion. All of that is real and all of that is valid. Do not let anyone tell you how to feel. But when you are ready, and it does not have to be today or tomorrow, I want you to make the same decision I made.

Decide that this disease will not write your ending.

Then start doing the work. Build your team, your neurologist, your physical therapist, your family. Build your protocol, research the supplements, talk to other patients, find what works for your body. Build your routine, the daily habits that keep you moving, keep you nourished, keep you connected.

You have more control than they told you. The research is evolving. The supplements are improving. The community is growing. And every single day that you show up and fight is a day that the statistics did not predict.

I will not promise you a cure, and I will not promise you that my protocol will work for you the way it has worked for me. But I will promise you this: giving up guarantees the outcome they predicted, while fighting gives you a chance at a different one.

Choose the fight. I did, and I am still here.
