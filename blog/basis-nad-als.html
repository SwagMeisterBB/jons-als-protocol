What Is NAD+ and Why Does It Matter

NAD+ stands for nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide. It is a coenzyme found in every cell in your body, and it is essential for producing cellular energy. Without NAD+, your mitochondria cannot convert the food you eat into the energy your cells need to function. It is that fundamental.

Here is the problem: NAD+ levels decline as you age. By the time you are in your 60s, your NAD+ levels may be half of what they were in your 20s. And in neurodegenerative diseases like ALS, that decline appears to be accelerated. Your motor neurons are already under attack. If they are also running low on the fuel they need to function, that is a compounding problem.

NAD+ is also involved in DNA repair through a family of enzymes called sirtuins. When NAD+ drops, your cells lose the ability to repair damage efficiently. In a disease like ALS where cellular stress is constant, that repair mechanism matters.

Why I Take Basis

  • It also contains pterostilbene, a polyphenol related to resveratrol but with better bioavailability. Pterostilbene activates sirtuins, which are the enzymes that use NAD+ to repair DNA and regulate cellular stress.

What the Research Says

I want to be clear: there are no large-scale clinical trials testing Basis or NR specifically in ALS patients. The research I rely on comes from aging science and neuroscience, not ALS-specific trials.

A 2017 clinical trial published in Nature Communications by Elysium showed that Basis safely and sustainably elevated NAD+ levels in healthy middle-aged adults. Participants taking the recommended dose saw a roughly 40% increase in NAD+ levels after four weeks.

In the neuroscience space, a 2018 study in Cell Reports demonstrated that NR supplementation improved mitochondrial function and extended lifespan in a mouse model of a neurodegenerative disease similar to ALS. The researchers found that boosting NAD+ levels protected motor neurons and delayed symptom onset.

Another line of research focuses on the role of NAD+ in axonal degeneration. A 2017 paper in Science showed that NAD+ metabolism is directly involved in the process by which axons (the long projections of nerve cells) degenerate. When NAD+ levels drop below a critical threshold, it triggers a self-destruction pathway in the axon. Maintaining NAD+ levels may help keep that threshold from being crossed.

Is this proof that Basis helps ALS? No. But the mechanism is sound, the safety profile is excellent, and the underlying biology points in a direction that makes sense for neurodegeneration. That meets my threshold for inclusion.
