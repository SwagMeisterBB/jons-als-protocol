L-Serine for ALS: What the Research Says and Why Jon Takes It

By Jon Edy • March 2026 • 5 min read

If Jon could only take one supplement for ALS, it would be L-Serine.

L-Serine is a naturally occurring amino acid that your body uses to make proteins, cell membranes, and other amino acids. It's found in foods like soybeans, nuts, eggs, and meat. It has FDA GRAS status (Generally Recognized as Safe), meaning it's considered safe for consumption.

But what caught Jon's attention - and what catches the attention of many ALS patients - is a clinical trial that showed remarkable results.

The Research

In a Phase 1 clinical trial, ALS patients who took 30 grams of L-Serine per day showed roughly 85% slower functional decline compared to historical controls. The trial was small, and Phase 1 trials are primarily designed to test safety rather than efficacy. But the results were striking enough to get attention in the ALS community.

The research originated from Dr. Paul Cox, an ethnobotanist who studied the unusually high rates of ALS-like disease in Guam. He traced it to a neurotoxin called BMAA, found in cycad seeds that were part of the local diet. L-Serine appeared to compete with BMAA, potentially blocking its toxic effects.

How Jon Takes It

Jon takes 30 grams of L-Serine per day, split into two doses:

  • 15 grams in the morning, mixed into a drink
  • 15 grams in the evening, mixed into a drink

It dissolves reasonably well in water and has a slightly sweet taste. Jon has taken it consistently for years.

Important Notes

  • The Phase 1 trial was small and primarily measured safety, not efficacy. Larger trials are needed.
  • L-Serine is an amino acid, not a drug. It is not FDA-approved as a treatment for ALS.
  • Jon's neurologist is aware that he takes L-Serine and supports it. Always consult your physician.
  • 30 grams per day is a high dose. Start with a conversation with your doctor.
