Vitamin D3 for ALS: Why It's One of My Foundation Supplements

Jon EdyMarch 29, 2026 · 5 min read

In this article

  1. Why I Call It a Foundation Supplement
  2. What the Research Says
  3. How I Take It
  4. Important Notes

If someone asked me which three supplements to start with after an ALS diagnosis, Vitamin D3 would be on that list. Not because it is the most exciting or the most researched for ALS specifically. But because the data on deficiency is so clear, the fix is so simple, and the downside of not addressing it is too significant to ignore.

I have spent thousands of hours reading research on ALS supplements. Some of them have thin evidence. Some have promising mechanisms but no clinical data. Vitamin D3 is different. The evidence that ALS patients are overwhelmingly deficient is not debatable. And the evidence that deficiency accelerates decline is growing.

Why I Call It a Foundation Supplement

After an ALS diagnosis, most patients become less active. They spend more time indoors. Sun exposure drops. And with it, Vitamin D production drops. This is not a theoretical problem. It shows up in bloodwork consistently.

I call Vitamin D3 a "foundation" supplement because it supports systems that ALS attacks from multiple angles: immune function, calcium regulation in neurons, and neuroprotection. You do not build a house without a foundation. You do not build a supplement protocol without addressing a deficiency that affects 80% of ALS patients.

What the Research Says

The research here is straightforward. Karam et al. (2013) found that 81% of ALS patients were either Vitamin D deficient or insufficient. That is not a small number. That is the vast majority of people living with this disease walking around with levels too low for basic physiological function.

Caire et al. (2016) took it a step further. They found that ALS patients with Vitamin D deficiency had faster functional decline as measured by the ALSFRS-R scale. Lower D levels correlated with faster progression. That is not proof of causation, but the association is strong enough that ignoring it seems reckless to me.

The mechanism makes biological sense. Vitamin D modulates the immune system, and immune dysregulation is a known feature of ALS. It regulates calcium homeostasis, and calcium dysfunction in motor neurons is part of how ALS damages cells. And there is preclinical evidence suggesting direct neuroprotective effects.

How I Take It

  • Form: Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol), not D2. D3 is more effective at raising blood levels.
  • Dose: 2,000 IU daily.
  • Timing: With breakfast. D3 is fat-soluble, so I take it with a meal that includes some fat.
  • Brand: Any reputable D3 supplement. This is a commodity vitamin. I do not overthink the brand.
  • Testing: I get my levels checked every 6 months. My target range is 50 to 80 ng/mL.

When I started, my levels were in the low 20s. After 3 months of 2,000 IU daily, I was at 62 ng/mL. I have stayed in that range since.

Important Notes

  • Get tested before you start. Vitamin D is one of the few supplements where toxicity is a real concern at very high doses. A simple blood test tells you where you are.
  • Work with your doctor on dosing. 2,000 IU is what works for me. Your optimal dose depends on your current levels, body weight, and how much sun exposure you get.
  • This is an easy conversation with your neurologist. Unlike some supplements, doctors are very comfortable testing and monitoring Vitamin D. It is standard care. If you are nervous about bringing up supplements, start with this one.
  • I am not a doctor. This is what I take and why. Consult your physician before starting any supplement.

Of all the supplements in my protocol, Vitamin D3 is the one I would call a no-brainer. The deficiency rates are overwhelming. The fix is cheap. The monitoring is easy. And the potential downside of staying deficient is real. I have been taking it for over 6 years and I have no intention of stopping.
