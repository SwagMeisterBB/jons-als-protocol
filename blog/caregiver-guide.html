I See You

If you're reading this, you're probably not the one with ALS. You're the one staying up late researching, ordering supplements, organizing pill boxes, and trying to hold it together. I see you. My family does this for me, and I don't take it for granted.

My wife, my sons, my whole family - they've been with me every step of this journey. They're the ones who drove to the health food store at 9 PM because I ran out of L-Serine. They're the ones who sat in the neurologist's office taking notes while I was still processing what I'd just been told. They're the ones who researched supplements when I was too tired or too frustrated to do it myself.

So if you're here because someone you love has ALS and you're trying to help - I wrote this page for you. Not for the patient. For you. Because I know that in most ALS households, the caregiver is the one doing the heavy lifting when it comes to research, supplements, and daily management. And nobody talks about that enough.

You don't need to understand every study or memorize every dosage, you just need a system that works. That's what I'm going to help you build.

How to Use My Protocol as a Caregiver

I've set up my protocol to work for caregivers as much as patients. Here's how I'd suggest you approach it:

Start with the free blog posts

Before you spend a dollar, read through the blog. I've written individual posts on the key supplements - L-Serine, HMB, Vitamin D3, and others. Each one explains what the supplement does, why I take it, and what the research says. This gives you the foundation to understand the "why" behind everything in the protocol.

Understanding the reasoning matters because when your loved one's doctor asks "why are you taking this?" - and they will ask - you want to be able to explain it clearly. The blog posts give you that language.

The full protocol PDF is designed to be printed

I made the full protocol as a PDF specifically so you can print it out and bring it to appointments. It includes every supplement, every dosage, the research behind each one, and the daily schedule. Hand it to the neurologist. Let them read it. Most doctors appreciate when patients and families come prepared with organized information rather than a scattered list of things they read online.

The PDF is also organized so you can share specific sections with different people - the supplement list for whoever's ordering, the schedule for whoever's organizing the daily routine, the research summaries for the medical team.

The cheat sheet is built for you

Inside the protocol, there's a caregiver cheat sheet. It's a single-page reference with the daily supplement schedule broken into morning, afternoon, and evening. No research citations, no long explanations - just what to give, when to give it, and whether it needs to be taken with food. Print it. Tape it to the cabinet. That's what it's for.

What to Prioritize if You're Overwhelmed

Here's what I tell every caregiver who reaches out to me: do not try to start everything at once.

My full protocol includes around 20 supplements, and I didn't start taking all 20 on day one. I built up over months. If you look at the full list and feel overwhelmed, that's normal. You're not behind and you're not failing. You're being smart by not rushing.

The Foundation Three

If I could only take three supplements, these are the three I'd start with:

  • L-Serine - This is the backbone of my protocol. The research on L-Serine and ALS is the most compelling I've found, and it's the supplement I've taken the longest. I take 15 grams twice a day.
  • Vitamin D3 - Most ALS patients are deficient. It's inexpensive, widely available, and important for bone and immune health. I take 5,000 IU daily.
  • HMB (beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate) - This supports muscle preservation. For ALS patients losing muscle, HMB is one of the most practical supplements to add early.

Start there. Get comfortable with the routine. Make it part of the daily rhythm. Then, when you're ready, add the next one. And the next. There's no deadline. The goal is consistency, not speed.

Build the routine before building the stack

The hardest part of managing supplements isn't knowing which ones to take but actually taking them every single day. Get a good pill organizer, set phone alarms, and build it into the existing daily routine around breakfast, lunch, and dinner. Once the habit is locked in with three supplements, adding more is easy. If the habit isn't there, adding more just creates chaos.

I'd rather someone take three supplements every day for a year than try to take twenty and give up after a month.

Taking Care of Yourself Too

I need to say this directly, because I know the kind of person reading this page. You're the kind of person who puts everyone else first, who will research supplements until 2 AM and then wake up at 6 to start the day, and who says "I'm fine" when you're not fine.

Caregiver burnout is real, and it's not a weakness. It's what happens when a good person carries too much for too long without help.

You can't pour from an empty cup. I need you healthy. My family needs me to say this to you: take care of yourself too.

Here are resources that exist specifically for ALS caregivers:

  • The ALS Association - They have a dedicated caregiver support section with resources, support groups, and a helpline.
  • Local ALS support groups - Most ALS Association chapters run caregiver-specific groups. These aren't just for venting. They're where you learn the practical stuff that nobody else can teach you - from people who actually understand.
  • Respite care - If you haven't looked into respite care, do it now. Taking a break isn't abandoning your person. It's how you keep being able to show up for them.
  • Your own doctor - When was the last time you had a checkup? I'm asking because I care about you, not just the person you're caring for.

You found this page because you love someone with ALS. That love is the most powerful thing in any protocol. But it has to include love for yourself too.
