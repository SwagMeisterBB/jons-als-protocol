CoQ10 for ALS: Mitochondrial Support That Makes Sense to Me

Jon Edy, ALS Patient & Protocol Author

ALS does not just attack motor neurons from one angle. It hits them from several directions at once. One of those directions is mitochondrial dysfunction. Your mitochondria are the power plants inside every cell, and when they fail, the cell dies. Motor neurons are especially vulnerable because they are large cells with enormous energy demands. CoQ10 is one of the most direct ways I know to support mitochondrial function, and I have been taking it for years.

Why Mitochondrial Support Matters

Mitochondrial dysfunction is not a fringe theory in ALS research but one of the established mechanisms of motor neuron death. The mitochondria in ALS patients show structural abnormalities, impaired electron transport chain function, and increased production of reactive oxygen species. In plain terms: the power plants are broken, they produce less energy, and they leak toxic byproducts.

CoQ10, also known as Coenzyme Q10, is essential for ATP production because it sits in the mitochondrial membrane and shuttles electrons through the energy production chain. Without adequate CoQ10, cells cannot produce energy efficiently. And here is the problem: CoQ10 levels naturally decline with age and decline further with disease, so by the time most people are diagnosed with ALS, they are already running a deficit in the one molecule their mitochondria need most.

This is not a magic bullet. But the logic is straightforward. If mitochondrial dysfunction is part of what kills motor neurons, and CoQ10 is essential for mitochondrial function, then maintaining adequate levels seems like a reasonable thing to do.

What the Research Says

The clinical data on CoQ10 in ALS is limited but informative. Ferrante et al. (2005) ran a Phase II trial using high-dose CoQ10 at 2,700mg per day in ALS patients. The trial did not reach statistical significance on its primary endpoint, but there was a trend toward slower decline in the ALSFRS-R score in the CoQ10 group compared to placebo. The study was not powered to detect a small effect, so a negative result does not mean no effect; it means the effect, if present, was not large enough to detect with that sample size.

Matthews et al. (1998) demonstrated neuroprotective effects of CoQ10 in neurotoxin models. Animals treated with CoQ10 showed significantly less neuronal damage when exposed to compounds that mimic neurodegenerative disease mechanisms. This is preclinical data, not human data. But it supports the biological rationale.

I want to be direct about where the evidence stands. The ALS-specific clinical trial used a much higher dose than I take and it did not hit its primary endpoint. I am not taking CoQ10 because a clinical trial proved it works for ALS. I am taking it because the mechanism is sound, the safety profile is excellent, and mitochondrial support is a rational part of a broader protocol.

How I Take It

  • Form: Ubiquinol, not ubiquinone. Ubiquinol is the reduced, active form. After age 40, the body becomes less efficient at converting ubiquinone to ubiquinol, so I skip that step and take the active form directly.
  • Dose: 200 to 400mg daily. I adjust based on how I feel and what else I am taking.
  • Timing: With breakfast. CoQ10 is fat-soluble, so it needs dietary fat for absorption. I take it with a meal that includes eggs or avocado.
  • Cost: Roughly $30 to $40 per month for quality ubiquinol at this dose range.

The distinction between ubiquinol and ubiquinone matters. Most cheap CoQ10 supplements are ubiquinone, and your body has to convert that to ubiquinol before it can use it. That conversion becomes less efficient as you age and even less efficient when you are dealing with a disease that already stresses your cellular machinery, so pay the extra for ubiquinol. It is worth it.

Important Notes

  • Very well tolerated. CoQ10 has an excellent safety profile. Side effects are rare and usually limited to mild GI discomfort at very high doses. I have never had an issue.
  • The clinical trial at 2,700mg did not hit its primary endpoint. I take a lower dose as general mitochondrial support, not as a treatment for ALS. There is a difference between treating a disease and supporting the biology that the disease attacks.
  • If you take statins, you need CoQ10. Statins deplete CoQ10 as a direct side effect of their mechanism of action. If your doctor has you on a statin and you are not supplementing CoQ10, you are running your mitochondria at a further disadvantage. This applies to everyone, not just ALS patients.
  • I am not a doctor. This is what I take and why. Consult your physician before starting any supplement.

CoQ10 is not the most exciting supplement in my protocol and it does not have a dramatic clinical trial behind it. But it has something better for my purposes: a clear, well-understood mechanism that directly addresses one of the pathways ALS uses to destroy motor neurons. I have been taking it for years, my neurologist is comfortable with it, and at $30 to $40 a month it is one of the easier decisions in my stack.
