If you understand one thing about ALS at the cellular level, understand this: oxidative stress is one of the primary ways motor neurons die. Your cells produce reactive oxygen species as a normal byproduct of metabolism. Healthy cells neutralize them. In ALS, that system is overwhelmed. The neurons cannot keep up, and the damage accumulates until the cell dies. Glutathione is the body's most important defense against this process, and NAC is how I keep my glutathione levels up.

Why Antioxidants Matter for ALS

Oxidative stress is not a minor player in ALS but one of the core mechanisms. This is especially clear in familial ALS cases involving mutations in the SOD1 gene, which stands for superoxide dismutase 1 and is literally an antioxidant enzyme. When it is mutated, the cell loses a critical piece of its defense system. But oxidative stress is not limited to SOD1 cases; it shows up across all forms of ALS, sporadic and familial alike.

Glutathione is often called the body's "master antioxidant," and it earns that title. It is present in every cell, where it neutralizes reactive oxygen species, regenerates other antioxidants like Vitamin C and E, and plays a role in detoxification. In ALS patients, glutathione levels are depleted because the demand exceeds the supply, and motor neurons that are already under siege lose one of their most important protective mechanisms.

This is not a subtle finding. The oxidative stress connection in ALS is one of the most well-documented aspects of the disease, and the question is not whether it matters but what you can do about it.

NAC vs. Glutathione: What I Actually Take

Here is where it gets practical. You cannot just swallow a glutathione capsule and expect much to happen. Oral glutathione has poor bioavailability. Your gut breaks it down before most of it reaches your bloodstream. Some people use liposomal glutathione or IV glutathione to get around this, and I occasionally use the liposomal form as a direct supplement. But my primary strategy is different.

NAC, or N-Acetyl Cysteine, is a precursor to glutathione, which means your body uses it to make glutathione on its own. NAC is well absorbed orally, it has decades of safety data behind it, and it is cheap. So rather than trying to force glutathione into my system through a route that does not work well, I give my body the raw material it needs to produce its own.

Beretta et al. (2003) demonstrated that NAC supplementation increased glutathione levels in ALS patients. That is exactly what you want to see. The precursor gets in, the body uses it, and glutathione goes up. Louwerse et al. (1995) ran a clinical trial of NAC in ALS patients. The results were mixed in terms of clinical outcomes, but the safety profile was good. NAC did not cause problems, even in a disease population.

I am not claiming that NAC will slow ALS progression. What I am saying is that oxidative stress is a documented mechanism of motor neuron death, glutathione is the primary defense against it, ALS patients are depleted, and NAC reliably raises glutathione levels. The logic chain is clear.

How I Take It

  • Primary: 600mg NAC, twice daily. I take it on an empty stomach for better absorption, typically 30 minutes before meals.
  • Secondary: Liposomal glutathione occasionally as a direct supplement. Not daily, but a few times per week when I feel like I need extra support.
  • Cost: NAC runs about $15 to $20 per month. It is one of the cheapest supplements in my entire protocol.

The empty stomach part matters with NAC because food can reduce absorption. I take my morning dose before breakfast and my evening dose before dinner, and it is a simple routine that is easy to maintain.

Important Notes

  • GI upset is possible. NAC can cause nausea or stomach discomfort in some people, especially at higher doses. If this happens, start with 600mg once daily and build up over a week or two. Taking it with a small amount of food is better than not taking it at all.
  • Nitroglycerin interaction. If you take nitroglycerin for heart conditions, talk to your doctor before starting NAC. There is a known interaction that can cause dangerous drops in blood pressure.
  • The mucolytic benefit is actually useful for ALS. NAC was originally developed as a mucus-thinning medication. For ALS patients who deal with thick secretions and respiratory challenges, this is not a side effect. It is a bonus. NAC helps thin mucus in the airways, which can make breathing and coughing more effective.
  • I am not a doctor. This is what I take and why. Consult your physician before starting any supplement.

NAC is one of those supplements that does multiple useful things at once: it raises glutathione to fight oxidative stress, thins mucus to help with respiratory symptoms, has decades of safety data, and costs less than a coffee per day. When I look at the full picture of what ALS does to the body and what NAC addresses, it is one of the clearest decisions in my protocol.
