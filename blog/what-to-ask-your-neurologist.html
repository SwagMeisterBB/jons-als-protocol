When I first started researching supplements after my ALS diagnosis, I felt a strange kind of guilt about it. Like I was going behind my doctor's back. I'd read about L-Serine or creatine and think, "This sounds promising, but what is my neurologist going to say?"

If you've felt that same hesitation, you're not alone. Many ALS patients I've talked to over the years share it. But here's what I've learned: the conversation about supplements doesn't have to be adversarial. In fact, it can become one of the most productive parts of your care, if you approach it the right way.

Important: Nothing in this article is medical advice. I'm an ALS patient sharing what has worked for me in my own doctor-patient relationship. Your situation is different from mine, and your neurologist knows your case better than I do.

Why This Conversation Matters

When I was first diagnosed, I did what a lot of us do: I went down the research rabbit hole. PubMed studies at 2 AM, ALS forums, YouTube videos. I was building a supplement list before I'd even had a real conversation with my neurologist about it.

That was a mistake, not because the research was wrong, but because I was making decisions in isolation. My neurologist had information I didn't: what medications I was on, what my bloodwork looked like, what other patients had experienced.

The reality is that most neurologists won't bring up supplements on their own, not necessarily because they're against them, but because the evidence base for most supplements in ALS is limited and doctors are trained to be cautious. They don't want to give false hope.

But that doesn't mean they won't engage with you about it. In my experience, when you bring the conversation to them thoughtfully and transparently, most doctors are willing to listen and some are even genuinely curious.

The goal isn't to convince your neurologist that supplements will cure ALS. The goal is to make sure that whatever you're taking is safe alongside your other treatments, and that your doctor is a partner in your decisions rather than being left in the dark.

Questions I Wish I'd Asked Sooner

If I could go back to my early appointments, here are the questions I'd walk in with. I've shared these with other ALS patients, and many have told me they changed the tone of their appointments entirely.

  • "Are there any supplements that could interact with my medications?" This is the single most important question. Some supplements can interfere with riluzole or other medications. Your neurologist needs the full picture to keep you safe.
  • "What's your stance on L-Serine given the Phase 1 trial results?" This tells your doctor you've done your homework. You're not asking them to validate a random internet claim, you're referencing a published clinical trial. It opens a very different kind of conversation.
  • "Would you be open to monitoring my bloodwork if I add supplements?" This is a collaboration question, and in my experience, doctors love it. You're saying: I want to do this responsibly, and I want you involved. Most neurologists will agree to periodic bloodwork to watch for anything unusual.
  • "Are there supplements I should definitely avoid?" Some supplements can be harmful for ALS patients specifically, or can interfere with clinical trials you might want to join later. Your neurologist may have strong opinions here, and those opinions are worth hearing.
  • "What would you want to know if I'm taking supplements on my own?" This is a powerful question because it hands the conversation to your doctor. It says: I'm being transparent, and I want to give you whatever information you need. In my experience, this question alone can transform a tense appointment into a collaborative one.

You don't have to ask all of these in one visit. Pick the ones that feel most relevant to where you are right now. The point is to open the door.

How to Bring a Supplement List to Your Appointment

One thing I learned the hard way: don't just mention supplements in passing. Come prepared. Here's what I do now before every neurology appointment.

Print out your supplement list. I bring a simple printed sheet with three columns: the supplement name, the brand, and the daily dose. My neurologist keeps a copy in my chart. It takes five minutes to prepare and it makes the conversation so much easier.

Include the specific brands. Not all supplements are created equal. A generic "fish oil" tells your doctor very little. The brand and dose matter because quality varies, and your doctor may want to look up specific formulations.

Be completely transparent. If you're taking something your doctor might question, put it on the list anyway because hiding supplements from your neurologist is like hiding symptoms: it only hurts you. I've had moments where I felt sheepish about something on my list, but my neurologist's response was always more measured than I expected.

Note when you started each one. This helps your doctor correlate any changes in your bloodwork or symptoms with what you've added or removed. It's the kind of detail that turns your supplement use into useful clinical data.

Bring any research you found compelling. You don't need to bring a stack of printed studies, but if there's a specific trial or paper that influenced your decision, mention it. My neurologist appreciated knowing where I was getting my information because it helped him understand my reasoning and, in some cases, point me toward better sources.

What I Learned from My Own Neurologist

I'll be honest: the first time I brought up supplements, my neurologist was cautious. Not dismissive, cautious. He said something I'll never forget: "I can't tell you these will help, but I can help you make sure they don't hurt."

That became the foundation of our approach. Over the years, our conversations about supplements have become a regular part of my care. Here's what I learned through that process.

My neurologist was most supportive of L-Serine. The Phase 1 trial data gave him something concrete to evaluate, and the safety profile was reassuring. He told me that if I was going to take any supplement, L-Serine had the most interesting evidence behind it. That was validating to hear.

He was more cautious about high-dose antioxidants. He explained that some antioxidants can interfere with cellular processes in ways we don't fully understand, especially at high doses. He didn't tell me to stop, but he asked me to keep the doses moderate and let him track things through bloodwork.

He was neutral on creatine, he'd seen the mixed trial results but acknowledged the theoretical rationale for muscle energy support. His position was essentially: "It probably won't hurt, and it might help. Let's keep an eye on your kidney function."

The biggest shift came when I started being fully transparent. Early on, I'd mentioned supplements almost apologetically, but once I started bringing my printed list, asking specific questions, and treating it like a normal part of my care, the whole dynamic changed. My neurologist started asking me about new supplements I was considering, and the relationship became a partnership.

The best doctor-patient relationships in ALS aren't the ones where the patient blindly follows orders or the doctor dismisses everything outside conventional treatment. They're the ones where both people come to the table with honesty and a shared goal: do whatever we can, as safely as we can.

If you haven't had this conversation with your neurologist yet, I encourage you to start. Bring your list. Ask your questions. You might be surprised at how it goes.
